Monoidal Categories

James Leslie - Monoidal Categories
Posted on April 15, 2018 by James Leslie

In mathematics, we are familiar with putting structure on sets, for example a group is a set with a binary operation satisfying certain properties. We can extend this notion to categories. The first examples I came across in my studies were monoidal categories, which are used to abstract the notion of a tensor product, and have deep ties to quantum mechanics and physics.

A monoidal category is a category 𝒞\mathcal{C} with a functor ⊗:𝒞×𝒞→𝒞 \otimes: \mathcal{C} \times \mathcal{C} \rightarrow \mathcal{C}, an object I∈𝒞 I \in \mathcal{C} called the unit and the following natural isomorphisms, written in terms of their components:

  1. An associator αA,B,C:(A⊗B)⊗C→A⊗(B⊗C)\alpha_{A,B,C}: (A \otimes B) \otimes C \rightarrow A \otimes (B \otimes C),
  2. A left unitor λA:I⊗A→A\lambda_A: I \otimes A \rightarrow A,
  3. A right unitor ρA:A⊗I→A\rho_A: A \otimes I \rightarrow A.

These maps all have to satisfy the following diagrams, aptly named the triangle and pentagon identities.

These two diagrams remarkably lead to a coherence theorem which states that every well formed formula built from α,λ,ρ\alpha, \lambda, \rho and their inverses holds. This gives us for free that ρI=λI \rho_I = \lambda_I, which would otherwise be very painful to prove.

The classic example of a monoidal category is 𝐇𝐢𝐥𝐛 \mathbf{Hilb}, the category of Hilbert spaces with morphisms as bounded linear maps. The tensor functor simply gives the tensor product of two Hilbert spaces and the unit object is ℂ \mathbb{C}, the complex numbers. The natural transformations are given in the obvious way. We can turn 𝐒𝐞𝐭 \mathbf{Set} into a monoidal category by having the ⊗ \otimes functor give the Cartesian product of two sets and having the terminal object 1 as the unit.

The study of both 𝐇𝐢𝐥𝐛 \mathbf{Hilb} and 𝐒𝐞𝐭 \mathbf{Set} are useful for physics. The former is used to formalize quantum mechanics and the latter classical physics. One property that 𝐇𝐢𝐥𝐛\mathbf{Hilb} has that 𝐒𝐞𝐭\mathbf{Set} doesn’t is the existence of a zero object. This is an object, denoted 00, which is both initial and terminal, that is, given any other object A A, there is exactly one morphism 0→A 0 \rightarrow A and exactly one morphism A→0 A \rightarrow 0. It isn’t hard to see that 𝐇𝐢𝐥𝐛 \mathbf{Hilb}, the zero object is simply the Hilbert space consisting of just a zero vector. It is also not difficult to show that 𝐒𝐞𝐭 \mathbf{Set} doesn’t have a zero object, since if it did it would need to be isomorphic to both the one element set and the empty set, which is impossible.

It is easy to see that in 𝐇𝐢𝐥𝐛 \mathbf{Hilb}, we have 0⊗0≅0 0 \otimes 0 \cong 0. This is true for all monoidal categories with a zero object, as we shall now prove. I highly recommend trying this exercise for yourself first!

This is only the tip of the iceberg when it comes to monoidal categories, for example, we might want A⊗B≅B⊗A A \otimes B \cong B \otimes A, in which case we need to look at braided monoidal categories.
