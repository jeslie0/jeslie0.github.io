Yoneda, Yoneda, Yoneda

James Leslie - Yoneda, Yoneda, Yoneda
Posted on April 23, 2018 by James Leslie

The Yoneda Lemma is one of the few ‘theorems’ one learns in a first course on category theory and it is an invaluable tool.

Suppose we have a locally small category 𝒜 \mathcal{A}, then for any two objects A,B A, B there is a set of maps from A A to B B, denoted 𝒜(A,B) \mathcal{A}(A,B). We can make this into a functor by forgetting about the domain. We define the following functor:

HB:𝒜op→𝐒𝐞𝐭 H_B:\mathcal{A}^{op} \rightarrow \mathbf{Set},

such that A↦HB(A)=𝒜(A,B) A \mapsto H_B(A) = \mathcal{A}(A, B) and (f:A→C)↦(HA(f):HA(C)→HA(A))(f:A \rightarrow C) \mapsto (H_A(f):H_A(C) \rightarrow H_A(A)). Given any map g:C→A g:C \rightarrow A, (HA(f))(g)=g∘f:A→A (H_A(f))(g) = g \circ f:A \rightarrow A. We can go further however, by removing our choice of AA. We define a functor

H⋅:𝒜→[𝒜op,𝐒𝐞𝐭]H_\cdot:\mathcal{A} \rightarrow [\mathcal{A}^\text{op}, \mathbf{Set}],

where A↦HA A \mapsto H_A and (f:A→B)↦(Hf:HA→HB) (f:A \rightarrow B) \mapsto (H_f:H_A \rightarrow H_B), where Hf H_f is a natural transformation. For any C∈𝒜 C \in \mathcal{A}, the C C component of Hf H_f is a function (Hf)C:HA(C)→HB(C) (H_f)_C:H_A(C) \rightarrow H_B(C) such that for any map g:C→A g:C \rightarrow A, ((Hf)C)(g)=f∘g:C→B ((H_f)_C)(g) = f \circ g:C \rightarrow B.

The functor H⋅ H_\cdot is known as the Yoneda embedding. It comes hand in hand with the Yoneda Lemma.

Yoneda lemma

Let 𝒜\mathcal{A} be a locally small category. Then 

[𝒜op,𝐒𝐞𝐭](HA,X)≅X(A)[\mathcal{A}^\text{op}, \mathbf{Set}](H_A, X) \cong X(A),

naturally in A∈𝒜A \in \mathcal{A} and X∈[𝒜op,𝐒𝐞𝐭] X \in  [\mathcal{A}^\text{op}, \mathbf{Set}].

There are many discussions on the importance of Yoneda, however we shall skip over them and instead look at the Yoneda embedding. This functor can only really be called an embedding if it is both full and faithful. This is a trivial result of the Yoneda lemma, however it is not necessary to invoke the lemma and it can be proven without it.

Yoneda embedding is full and faithful

Let A,A′∈𝒜 A, A' \in \mathcal{A}, and let α:HA→HA′ \alpha:H_A \rightarrow H_{A'} be a natural transformation. We need to find a map f:A→A′ f:A \rightarrow A' such that α=Hf \alpha = H_f. Looking at the A A component of α \alpha gives us a function

αA:HA(A)→HA′(A)\alpha_A:H_A(A) \rightarrow H_{A'}(A).

We apply this to the identity map on A A to get a map αA(1A):A→A′ \alpha_A(1_A):A \rightarrow A'. I claim that this is the required map. For notation, we shall call it f f. It is the case that Hf=α H_f = \alpha if and only if each component is equal, that is (Hf)B=αB (H_f)_B = \alpha_B for all objects B∈𝒜 B \in \mathcal{A}. Choosing an arbitrary B∈𝒜 B \in \mathcal{A}, as both  (Hf)B (H_f)_B and αB \alpha_B are functions, they are equal if and only if they take the same values on the same inputs. Recall the domains and codomains of the functions:

(Hf)B,αB:HA(B)→HA′(B)(H_f)_B, \alpha_B: H_A(B) \rightarrow H_{A'}(B).

For faithfulness, again fix A,A′∈𝒜 A, A' \in \mathcal{A} and consider two natural transformations Hf,Hg:HA→HA′ H_f, H_g: H_A \rightarrow H_A'. Supposing Hf=Hg H_f = H_g, we need to show that f=g f=g. As Hf=Hg H_f = H_g, (Hf)A=(Hg)A) (H_f)_A = (H_g)_A). These are both functions, so we apply them to 1A 1_A giving us f∘1A=g∘1A f \circ 1_A = g \circ 1_A, therefore f=g f = g, proving faithfulness.

Despite not explicitly using the Yoneda lemma, everything we have done is used in the proof of it. There is one more result that helps capture the idea behind the proof for Yoneda.

Yoneda embedding is injective on isomorphism classes

Suppose for two objects A,B∈𝒜 A, B \in \mathcal{A} that HA≅HB H_A \cong H_{B}. This means that we have two natural isomorphisms:

α:HA→HB,β:HB→HA\alpha:H_A \rightarrow H_B, \qquad \beta:H_B \rightarrow H_A

which are inverses to each other. By reasoning from above, we a map αA(1A):A→B \alpha_A(1_A):A \rightarrow B and βB(1B):B→A \beta_B(1_B):B \rightarrow A. For notation, we shall call αA(1A)=f \alpha_A(1_A) = f and βB(1B)=g \beta_B(1_B) = g. Again from above, we see that αB(g)=αA(1A)∘g \alpha_B(g) = \alpha_A(1_A) \circ g. Substituting for g g gives that αB(βB(1B))=αA(1A)∘βB(1B) \alpha_B(\beta_B(1_B)) = \alpha_A(1_A) \circ \beta_B(1_B). As α \alpha and β \beta are inverses, their components are mutual inverses. This means αB(βB(1B))=1B \alpha_B(\beta_B(1_B)) = 1_B, so αA(1A)∘βB(1B)=1B \alpha_A(1_A) \circ \beta_B(1_B) = 1_B. A similar argument shows that βB(1B)∘αA(1A)=1A \beta_B(1_B) \circ \alpha_A(1_A) = 1_A, hence αA(1A) \alpha_A(1_A) and βB(1B) \beta_B(1_B) are isomorphisms. Therefore, A≅B A \cong B.
